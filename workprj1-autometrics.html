Overview

AutoMetrics is a startup company that leverages machine learning in the welding industry to increase production efficiency, catch defects earlier, and reduce rework cost. The dashboard software that I mainly worked on, replaces the eyes of welders, and is a centralized platform that helps welders better monitor and stay in control during each weld project. In brief, the dashboard monitors multiple welding jobs, provides proactive quality check, and users will get notified whenever something abnormal is being detected.

The Problem

Crowded Layout + Vague Information

The previous dashboard was able to measure a lot of important factors that can be crucial during the welding process, however due to the lack of visual hirearchy, poor structure, and uneven empty space all over the interface, components and information were all crowded together. Especially when most content are supposed to update constantly during live, the dashboard made users feel difficult to understand and rely on. Couple of the important and core features that were meant to be the selling point of the company were too vague for welders to understand thereby loose their value. Other details like naming consistency and visual elements needed to be unified and integrated. There was a lot of potential and space for improvement when the product was handed to me. We are aiming to bring users a reliable product that they can trust and monitor with during weld jobs, without physically being on site.

My Role:

The sole UX UI Designer / Video Producer / Brochure Designer / Website Designer

I was assigned to redesign the dashboard, focusing on creating clear hirearchy, and communicating to users with precise visual language that reflect live data, warnings, and insights. Another goal for me was to bring users an intuitive navigation experience, and reduced cognitive load time.

The 2 Users:

#1 The Welders #2 AutoMetrics Staff
The welders will be mainly observing ongoing welding projects through the dashboard, as well as checking back on finished welding projects from archive. AutoMetrics staff member will be setting up projects and onboarding welders to the dashboard.

Project Goals

  • - Allow users to easily track all the constantly updating data and feel in control over the predictions.
  • - Different sections need to inform users with messages based on what the core technology detects in backend.
  • - The dashboard needs to alert users when deviation is detected during welding.
  • - Effortless and intuitive navigation between dashboard pages.

The Challenges:

#1 Picking a starting point

While drafting out some possibilities for the dashboard, I was in a dilemma whether to work on designing the individual components first or work on the distribution of space first. The dashboard consists of individual components that have unique purposes, I started with designing the individual components first because each component had a different scenario, resulting in all kinds of layouts and formats. I wanted to explore the possibilities of different layout for each individual component and see how they mix and match together when incorporated. Later I realized that this was not a good approach, I ended up with all kinds of different design variations but when trying to fit them into one space, there were always trouble with consistency and a huge amount of changes needed to be made individually no matter how many variations I had for backup.
I shifted my focus to space distribution, I learned from my failure earlier that components were flexible enough to be reorganized and designed into many formats. This meant that I could plan a layout for the components according to their function and purpose, and distribute them to different areas first. Settling down on where to place all components worked well and later I still had a lot of freedom to design each component according to the amount of space they had.

#2 Optimizing content for users

Showing the right and sufficient amount of information on the limited amount of dashboard space was a challenge. Previously, there was redundant amount of information on the dashboard that users needed to process. The important and even alarming content were easily missed, users had a hard time to understand and keep track of all the constantly updating welding information. After understanding the purpose of each component, one challenge for me was to discuss with my project manager about what needed to be kept, how to deliver more precise messages, and reduce cognitive load. In order for important information to stand out and easy to understand, it needed to be amplified to catch attention with thoughtful structure and clear wordings. Even after removing a significant amount of excessive content, we realized that in order to bring users an informative yet organized interface, the remaining content still needed more than one page to fit on the dashboard. The decisions made in this phase opened up more design opportunities like exploring ways to distribute content onto two pages. This was a big milestone that had an impact on the whole monitor experience, and users' navigation experience.

Highlights During My Design Journey:

#1 Understanding individual components

In order to understand the meaning and function of each section, understand how and what sort of information needed to be delivered to welders, I listed down all the components and made a list of the purpose and function of each component to discuss with my manager, this really helped me understand how the dashboard currently operates, identify questions, and come up with quick ideas on how to improve. A brief understanding process led me to more meaningful meetings with the developers, and I was able to get answers, and discover underlying issues that developers had with the components.

#2 Learning from the industry

There were standard visual clues in the welding industry that welders see everyday and familiar with, for example warning signs, hazards and specific welding related signs. We wished to include these signs in our dashboard to easily help welders understand potential problems with signs that they are already familiar with. This really helped me consolidate my design while creating purposeful visuals that caught users attention. I did research on how other welding companies utilize signs and language in their dashboard system, and was able to learn not only design elements, but also how other companies grouped relevant information in tabs in order to utilize the limited amount of space while letting important messages stand out. This was a big insight for me, and later made huge impact on how I redesigned sections.

#3 Connecting with developers

I gathered pain points from developers, and from my project manager while he was testing the dashboard with his clients. Understanding the core features from developers perspectives helped me understand how they imagine data getting delivered to users. This also helped me understand more about what kind of users journey users would be going through while viewing welding details on the dashboard.

The Result:

After 4 months of learning and development, the redesigned dashboard was completed and ready to be built and tested. The whole design covers users entire experience from signing up a new project to monitoring welding parts in live and reviewing finished projects. I also created documents explaining each section and all design elements such as button style and use of colours for both future developer and designers to reference. A site map was also created to make sure that team members are familiar with how different buttons lead to different pages, and how users will be navigating through all the pages. What I learned:

Establishing a set of goals for a project is important, it helped me stick with the most important intentions and problems especially while design can go into many directions. Do not get attached to any idea especially when experiments are not making much progress. I kept pushing myself to design new forms of the different components but no matter how I change the design, they never fit when I try to combine them all together onto the dashboard space. Later I tried a new approach which was to distribute the amount of space for each component first, Moving on to another plan not only refreshed my fogged up mindset but also fired up curiosity and the task became intriguing during the design process and opened up opportunities for problem solving. As a designer I will always be exploring new topics that I am not familiar with, finding inspiration and learning how other related companies design their products really inspired me, and kept me updated with industry standards while making my own decisions. One regret is that due to my contract length, I was not able to test the dashboard with real users, and I did not get the chance to improve based on users feedback from using the new interface.
